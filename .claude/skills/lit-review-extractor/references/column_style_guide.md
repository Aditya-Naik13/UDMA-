# Column Length & Style Guide

Targets below are measured from the reference CSV
(`Engineering & Safety-dominant AV discourse Literature Review`), so entries
for this section read consistently with it. These are ceilings, not quotas —
shorter and precise beats longer and padded.

| Column | Target | Hard cap |
|---|---|---|
| Paper Title | copy exactly as printed | — |
| Author(s) | single author: `Last`. Two or more authors: `Last et al.` (first author's last name only, no initials, no co-author names spelled out) | — |
| Year | 4 digits | — |
| Journal or Proceedings | as printed | — |
| DOI or URL | full link | — |
| Sub-theme | 2-5 words | 6 words |
| Central claim | 2 sentences | 60 words |
| Key concepts or framework | 2-3 short concept definitions | 60 words |
| Who is centered | 1 sentence | 30 words |
| Value foreclosed (community value left out) | 1 sentence, name the value(s) explicitly | 40 words |
| Relevance to argument | 2-3 sentences | 75 words |
| Gap or limitation | 1-2 sentences | 55 words |
| Quotable moment | 1 short exact quote + page cite | 25-word quote |
| Synthesis paragraph | 2-3 sentences, written last, after all other columns are filled | 60 words |

## Style rules

- **No em dash (—) anywhere, in any column.** Use a comma, colon, period,
  or "and" instead. Check your own output for this character before
  writing the row — if you catch yourself reaching for one, rewrite the
  clause instead of substituting a hyphen.
- Write in plain declarative sentences. Avoid throat-clearing openers like
  "This paper explores..." or "It is argued that..." — start with the
  claim itself.
- Prefer concrete nouns over abstractions where the paper supports it
  (e.g., "bus rerouting decisions" rather than "infrastructure changes").
- "Central claim" and "Relevance to argument" are the two columns doing the
  most citation-load-bearing work — spend your word budget there, keep
  "Gap or limitation" and "Who is centered" tighter.
- If a paper's fit to the section's specific claim is not obvious or is a
  stretch, don't paper over that in "Relevance to argument." Say plainly
  what the thinner or broader connection actually is (e.g. it supports the
  wider transportation equity project even if it doesn't speak to this
  section's claim directly) rather than forcing a strong-sounding sentence
  that overstates the fit. Still write the row: a well-justified thin
  connection is preferable to silently dropping the paper.

# Paper Context — Lens for This Literature Review Section

## Full paper thesis (for background only — not the immediate lens)

Autonomous vehicles are not simply transportation vehicles but infrastructure,
yet they typically arrive in communities without those communities' input,
carrying efficiency-driven values inherited from transportation engineering.
Drawing on six co-design workshops with food pantry users, volunteers,
system-level stakeholders, and experts, in which a speculative AV provocation
surfaced how participants reasoned about mobility, the paper finds that these
efficiency-driven values are misaligned with the community concerns that
actually govern mobility in this context: equitable access, relational
trust, and flexibility for variable need. This misalignment reflects a
broader pattern in how infrastructure-scale technologies arrive in
communities without their participation in shaping what the technology is
for. The paper introduces Mobility as Social Service, a concept grounded in
this data that reframes mobility as an ongoing, relational practice of
equitable provisioning, and offers concrete implications for designing
AV-related infrastructure in social service contexts.

## This section's specific claim (the immediate working lens)

Transportation equity and mobility justice literature showing that mobility
access has long been unevenly distributed, and that infrastructure arriving
without community participation has historical precedent of reproducing
exclusion.

When filling "Relevance to argument," write against THIS claim first (does
the paper support the "unevenly distributed" half, the "infrastructure
without participation reproduces exclusion" half, or both) — then, only if
natural, note how it also feeds the larger AV/Mobility as Social Service
argument. Don't force an AV connection onto a paper that has none; most
papers in this section won't mention AVs at all, and that's expected.

If a paper doesn't clearly speak to this section's specific claim, check
whether it's still relevant to the broader project (the full paper thesis
above: AVs as infrastructure, community values misaligned with
efficiency-driven design, Mobility as Social Service). If it is, say so
honestly in "Relevance to argument" and name the thinner or more indirect
connection rather than overstating fit with the immediate claim. Only treat
a paper as a true non-fit if it doesn't connect to either the section claim
or the broader project.

## The three community values (for "Value foreclosed")

Use these as the check against what a paper's framing leaves out, same as
the reference CSV did for the AV engineering-discourse section:

- **Equitable access** — who gets to use/reach mobility, and on what terms
- **Relational trust** — mobility as embedded in ongoing relationships, not
  a one-off transaction
- **Flexibility for variable need** — accommodating needs that don't fit a
  standardized, efficiency-optimized model

A paper doesn't need to foreclose all three. Often it forecloses none
directly (e.g., a paper that IS centered on community participation) — in
that case, "Value foreclosed" can be "None directly foreclosed" or can note
what the paper still leaves under-theorized.
